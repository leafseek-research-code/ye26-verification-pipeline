# Section 2: Transfer Theorems and Circuit Instantiations

## Section 2.1: Scripted Rebalancing

In this section, we formalize the scripted rebalancing described in Section 1.2. Its input is a parameter set, and its output is a sequence of depth-2 linear circuits computing $R_n$, one for each multiple $n$ of $k$.

Let $k\geq1$ and $H\geq1$ be integers, let $I$ be a map from $[H]$ to circuits computing $R_k$, let $Z>1$ be a real number, and let $\alpha,\beta\in(0,1)$ be real numbers. We call $\mathrm{X}=(k,H,I,Z,\alpha,\beta)$ a parameter set.

In our parameter sets, $I$ is denoted by an ordered pair $(\mathbf{c},\boldsymbol{\iota})$, where $\mathbf c=(c_0,\ldots,c_{L-1})$ is an integer array satisfying $0\leq c_0<\dots<c_{L-1}\leq2^k-1$, and $\boldsymbol{\iota}:[H]\to[L]$ is a map. This means $I(h)=S_k(c_{\boldsymbol{\iota}(h)})$, the Sergeev circuit of order $k$ with identifier $c_{\boldsymbol{\iota}(h)}$ [Ser22; AL25, Section 5.1].

For each identifier $c\in\{0,\ldots,2^k-1\}$, Appendix A.2 defines a depth-2 linear circuit $S_k(c)$ computing $R_k$ with $2^k$ middle-gate nodes. Equivalently, $S_k(c)$ gives a rank-1 decomposition of $R_k$:
$$
S_k(c)=\sum_{r=0}^{2^k-1}(U_{c,r},V_{c,r}^\textsf{T}),\qquad\text{s.t. }R_k=\sum_{r=0}^{2^k-1}U_{c,r}V_{c,r}^\textsf{T}.
$$
Here, $U_{c,r}$ and $V_{c,r}$ are vectors indexed by $\{0,1\}^k$. For a vector $w$ indexed by $\{0,1\}^k$, set
$$
\operatorname{wnnz}_{\gamma_0,\gamma_1}(w)
:=
\sum_{x\in\{0,1\}^k}\mathbf{1}[w_x\neq0](\gamma_0)^{k-\|x\|_1}(\gamma_1)^{\|x\|_1},
$$
where $\|x\|_1$ denotes the number of 1-bits in the index $x\in\{0,1\}^k$. This is the sum of Bernoulli weights of the indices of nonzero entries in $w$, with weights $\gamma_0$ and $\gamma_1$ assigned to 0-bits and 1-bits, respectively.

For a rank-1 term $(U_{c,r},V_{c,r}^\textsf{T})$, define its *raw tilt* $\theta_{c,r}$ as
$$
\theta_{c,r}:=
\log_Z
\left(
\frac{\operatorname{wnnz}_{1-\beta,\beta}(V_{c,r})}
     {\operatorname{wnnz}_{1-\alpha,\alpha}(U_{c,r})}
\right),
$$
and its *state offset* $\Delta_{c,r}$ as $\Delta_{c,r}:=\left\lfloor \theta_{c,r}+\frac12\right\rfloor$. Let

$$
\operatorname{clamp}_H(t):=
\begin{cases}
0,&t<0,\\
t,&0\le t\le H-1,\\
H-1,&t>H-1.
\end{cases}
$$

The circuits $C_{\mathrm{X}}(n,h)$, for multiples $n$ of $k$ and states $h\in[H]$, are defined recursively by
$$
C_{\mathrm{X}}(0,h):=([1],[1]),
$$
and, for $n\ge k$,
$$
C_{\mathrm{X}}(n,h)
:=
\sum_{r=0}^{2^k-1}
(U_{c_i,r},V_{c_i,r}^\textsf{T})
\otimes
C_{\mathrm{X}}\!\left(n-k,\operatorname{clamp}_H(h+\Delta_{c_i,r})\right),
\qquad i=\boldsymbol{\iota}(h).
$$
**Proposition 2.1.** For every parameter set $\mathrm{X}$ in the above format, every multiple $n$ of $k$, and every state $h\in[H]$, $C_{\mathrm{X}}(n,h)$ is a depth-2 linear circuit that computes $R_n$.

*Proof of Proposition 2.1.* This is because $C_{\mathrm X}(n,h)$ is the root circuit of a computation tree [AL25, Appendix B] for the base matrix $R_1$ with total weighted depth $n$. For a detailed proof, see Appendix A.3. $\square$

For a $k$-bit identifier $c\in\{0,\ldots,2^k-1\}$, put $\overline{c}:=2^k-1-c$, the $k$-bit complement of $c$.

We define a *transposition* operation on parameter sets. If $\mathrm{X}=(k,H,I,Z,\alpha,\beta)$ and $I=(\mathbf{c},\boldsymbol{\iota})$, we define $\mathbf{c}^\textsf{T}:=(\overline{c_{L-1}},\ldots,\overline{c_0})$, $\boldsymbol{\iota}^\textsf{T}(h):=L-1-\boldsymbol{\iota}(H-1-h)$, $I^\textsf{T}:=(\mathbf{c}^\textsf{T},\boldsymbol{\iota}^\textsf{T})$, and $\mathrm{X}^\textsf{T}:=(k,H,I^\textsf{T},Z,\beta,\alpha)$.

It can be checked from the definitions that applying this transposition twice returns the original parameter set: $(\mathrm{X}^\textsf{T})^\textsf{T}=\mathrm{X}$. Thus, this operation is involutive, as one would expect from a transposition.

We now claim that the circuit family generated by the transposed parameter set is indeed isomorphic to the transpose of the original circuit family. A key fact is that $S_k(c)^\textsf{T}\cong S_k(\overline{c})$; see Fact A.2.2.

**Proposition 2.2.** Suppose no $\theta_{c,r}$ appearing in $\mathrm{X}$ lies in $\mathbb Z+\frac12$. Then
$$
\begin{aligned}
C_{\mathrm{X}}(n,h)^\textsf{T}&\cong C_{\mathrm{X}^\textsf{T}}(n,H-1-h),\\
C_{\mathrm{X}^\textsf{T}}(n,h)^\textsf{T}&\cong C_{\mathrm{X}}(n,H-1-h),
\end{aligned}
$$
for every multiple $n$ of $k$ and every $h\in[H]$.

*Proof of Proposition 2.2.* See Appendix B.2. $\square$

\end{aligned}

## Section 2.3: The Transfer Theorems

For an integer $\mathfrak{c}\ge2$ and an index $x\in[\mathfrak{c}]^n$, define its *normalized frequency array* by
$$
\operatorname{freq}(x)_i:=\frac1n\cdot|\{j\in[n]:x_j=i\}|,\qquad i=0,\ldots,\mathfrak{c}-1.
$$
Thus $\operatorname{freq}(x)\in\Delta_{[\mathfrak{c}]}$. For $p\in\operatorname{freq}([\mathfrak c]^n)$, define the *frequency class* $\operatorname{FC}_n(p):=\{x\in[\mathfrak c]^n:\operatorname{freq}(x)=p\}$.

For an integer $\mathfrak{c}\ge2$ and a probability distribution $p=(p_0,\ldots,p_{\mathfrak{c}-1})\in\Delta_{[\mathfrak{c}]}$, write
$$
\mathrm H_\mathfrak{c}(p):=-\sum_{i=0}^{\mathfrak{c}-1}p_i\log_2 p_i,\qquad 0\log_2 0:=0.
$$
For $p\in[0,1]$, we also write $\mathrm h(p):=\mathrm H_2(\operatorname{Ber}(p))=-p\log_2 p-(1-p)\log_2(1-p)$.

**Definition 2.3 (Normalized Log-Degree Density Functions).** Fix any matrix $M\in\mathbb{Z}^{\mathfrak{c}_0\times\mathfrak{c}_1}$ with no identically zero row or column. Index its rows by $[\mathfrak c_0]=\{0,\ldots,\mathfrak c_0-1\}$ and its columns by $[\mathfrak c_1]=\{0,\ldots,\mathfrak c_1-1\}$.

If $C=(A,B)$ is a depth-2 linear circuit computing $M^{\otimes k}$ for some $k\ge1$, let $L_C(x):=\operatorname{nnz}(A_{x,*})$ denote the degree of input gate $x$ in $C$, and let $R_C(y):=\operatorname{nnz}(B_{*,y})$ denote the degree of output gate $y$ in $C$. Define
$$
\begin{aligned}
f_C(p)
&:=\frac1k\,\mathbb E_{X\sim p^k}\bigl[\log_2 L_C(X)\bigr],&p\in\text{probability distribution on }[\mathfrak{c}_0].\\[4pt]
g_C(q)
&:=\frac1k\,\mathbb E_{Y\sim q^k}\bigl[\log_2 R_C(Y)\bigr],&q\in\text{probability distribution on }[\mathfrak{c}_1].
\end{aligned}
$$
We call $f_C$ and $g_C$ the left and right *normalized log-degree density functions* of $C$, respectively.

**Definition 2.4 (Manifestations of Circuits and Finite Circuit Collections).** Let $M\in\mathbb{Z}^{\mathfrak c_0\times \mathfrak c_1}$ be as in Definition 2.3.

If $C$ is a circuit computing $M^{\otimes k}$ for some $k\ge1$, define a map $P_C$ from $\Delta_{[\mathfrak{c}_0]}\times\Delta_{[\mathfrak{c}_1]}$ to the Cartesian plane by 
$$
P_C(p,q):=(f_C(p),g_C(q)).
$$
If $\mathcal{C}$ is a finite collection of depth-2 linear circuits $C$, each computing $M^{\otimes k}$ for some possibly different $k\ge1$, define a map $P_\mathcal{C}$ from $\Delta_{[\mathfrak{c}_0]}\times\Delta_{[\mathfrak{c}_1]}$ to convex polygons [[footnote 6]] on the Cartesian plane by
$$
P_\mathcal{C}(p,q):=\operatorname{conv}\{P_C(p,q):C\in\mathcal{C}\},
$$
the convex hull of the points $P_C(p,q)$ over all $C\in\mathcal C$.

We call $P_C(p,q)$ and $P_\mathcal{C}(p,q)$ the *manifestations* of $C$ and $\mathcal{C}$ at $(p,q)$, respectively.

**Theorem 2.5 (Realization of Convex-Hull Points).** Let $M\in\mathbb{Z}^{\mathfrak c_0\times \mathfrak c_1}$ be as in Definition 2.3, and let $\mathcal{C}$ be a finite circuit collection as in Definition 2.4.

For every $\varepsilon>0$, there exists $n_0\geq1$, depending only on $M$, $\mathcal{C}$, and $\varepsilon$, such that the following holds for every $n\geq n_0$. For every $(p,q)\in\operatorname{freq}([\mathfrak c_0]^n)\times\operatorname{freq}([\mathfrak c_1]^n)$ and every $(a,b)\in P_\mathcal{C}(p,q)$, there exists a depth-2 linear circuit $E_n$ that computes the submatrix $M^{\otimes n}[\operatorname{FC}_n(p),\operatorname{FC}_n(q)]$ and satisfies
$$
L_{E_n}(x)\le 2^{(a+\varepsilon)n},
\qquad
R_{E_n}(y)\le 2^{(b+\varepsilon)n},
$$
for every $x\in\operatorname{FC}_n(p)$ and every $y\in\operatorname{FC}_n(q)$.

*Proof of Theorem 2.5.* First suppose that $|\mathcal C|=2$, and let $\mathcal{C}=\{C_{\mathrm{hi}},C_{\mathrm{lo}}\}$. In this case, $(a,b)$ is a convex combination of $P_{C_{\mathrm{hi}}}(p,q)$ and $P_{C_{\mathrm{lo}}}(p,q)$. We form a tensor product of copies of $C_{\mathrm{hi}}$ and $C_{\mathrm{lo}}$ in the corresponding fraction of tensor coordinates, and then restrict the resulting circuit to $M^{\otimes n}[\operatorname{FC}_n(p),\operatorname{FC}_n(q)]$. By Hoeffding's inequality, except for a negligible fraction of the input and output gates, the input-gate degrees are at most $2^{an+n^{2/3}}$ and the output-gate degrees are at most $2^{bn+n^{2/3}}$. Removing these exceptional gates gives a circuit computing a “broken copy” of $M^{\otimes n}[\operatorname{FC}_n(p),\operatorname{FC}_n(q)]$. Finally, the hole-fixing lemma of [AL25] converts this broken copy into a full circuit, increasing the degrees by only a subexponential multiplicative factor.

For an arbitrary finite collection $\mathcal C$, start from $(a,b)$ and move southwest along $(a-t,b-t)$ until the boundary of the convex polygon $P_{\mathcal C}(p,q)$ is reached. The resulting boundary point must be a convex combination of $P_{C_{\mathrm I}}(p,q)$ and $P_{C_{\mathrm{II}}}(p,q)$ for some $C_{\mathrm I},C_{\mathrm{II}}\in\mathcal{C}$. Applying the two-circuit case to this boundary point directly gives upper bounds at least as strong as those required by the theorem. For a detailed proof, see Appendix B.2. $\square$

[[footnote 6]]: including degenerate polygons, such as a single point or a line segment.

### Section 2.3.1: The Size Transfer Theorem

**Theorem 2.6 (The Size Transfer Theorem).** Let $M\in\mathbb{Z}^{\mathfrak c_0\times \mathfrak c_1}$ be as in Definition 2.3, and let $\mathcal{C}$ be any finite circuit collection as in Definition 2.4. Then
$$
\sigma(M)\le2^{\xi(\mathcal{C})},\qquad\xi(\mathcal{C}):=
\max_{(p,q)\in\Delta_{[\mathfrak{c}_0]}\times\Delta_{[\mathfrak{c}_1]}}\left\{
\min_{(a,b)\in\operatorname{conv}\{P_C(p,q):C\in\mathcal C\}}
\max\{a+\text{H}_{\mathfrak{c}_0}(p),b+\text{H}_{\mathfrak{c}_1}(q)\}\right\}.
$$
*Proof of Theorem 2.6.* Build the circuit piece by piece. For each pair $(p,q)\in\operatorname{freq}([\mathfrak c_0]^n)\times\operatorname{freq}([\mathfrak c_1]^n)$, choose $(a,b)\in\operatorname{conv}\{P_C(p,q):C\in\mathcal C\}$ that minimizes $\max\{a+\mathrm H_{\mathfrak{c}_0}(p),b+\mathrm H_{\mathfrak{c}_1}(q)\}$. By Theorem 2.5, there exists a circuit computing $M^{\otimes n}[\operatorname{FC}_n(p),\operatorname{FC}_n(q)]$ with input-gate degrees at most $2^{(a+\varepsilon)n}$ and output-gate degrees at most $2^{(b+\varepsilon)n}$. Glue these circuits together by taking their concatenation sum. There are only polynomially many pairs of possible frequency arrays $(p,q)$, and the frequency classes satisfy $|\!\operatorname{FC}_n(p)|\le 2^{\mathrm H_{\mathfrak c_0}(p)n}$ and $|\!\operatorname{FC}_n(q)|\le 2^{\mathrm H_{\mathfrak c_1}(q)n}$. Hence the resulting circuit has size at most $2^{(\xi(\mathcal C)+\varepsilon)n+o(n)}$. Letting $n\to\infty$ and then $\varepsilon\downarrow0$ gives $\sigma(M)\le 2^{\xi(\mathcal C)}$. For a full proof, see Appendix B.2. $\square$

To see how $\mathcal{C}$ performs at different pairs of probability distributions $(p,q)$, define
$$
\xi(\mathcal{C},p,q):=\min_{(a,b)\in\operatorname{conv}\{P_C(p,q):C\in\mathcal C\}}
\max\{a+\text{H}_{\mathfrak{c}_0}(p),b+\text{H}_{\mathfrak{c}_1}(q)\}.
$$
We call the map $(p,q)\mapsto \xi(\mathcal{C},p,q)$ from $\Delta_{[\mathfrak{c}_0]}\times\Delta_{[\mathfrak{c}_1]}$ to $\mathbb{R}_{\geq0}$ the *size landscape* of $\mathcal{C}$.

### Section 2.3.2: The Degree Transfer Theorem

**Theorem 2.7 (The Degree Transfer Theorem).** Let $M\in\mathbb{Z}^{\mathfrak c_0\times \mathfrak c_1}$ be as in Definition 2.3, and let $\mathcal{C}$ be any finite circuit collection as in Definition 2.4. Then
$$
\delta(M)\le2^{\phi(\mathcal{C})},\qquad\phi(\mathcal{C}):=
\max_{(p,q)\in\Delta_{[\mathfrak{c}_0]}\times\Delta_{[\mathfrak{c}_1]}}\left\{
\min_{(a,b)\in\operatorname{conv}\{P_C(p,q):C\in\mathcal C\}}\max\{a,b\}\right\}.
$$
*Proof of Theorem 2.7.* Build the circuit piece by piece. For each pair $(p,q)\in\operatorname{freq}([\mathfrak c_0]^n)\times\operatorname{freq}([\mathfrak c_1]^n)$, choose $(a,b)\in\operatorname{conv}\{P_C(p,q):C\in\mathcal C\}$ that minimizes $\max\{a,b\}$. By Theorem 2.5, there exists a circuit computing $M^{\otimes n}[\operatorname{FC}_n(p),\operatorname{FC}_n(q)]$ with input-gate degrees at most $2^{(a+\varepsilon)n}$ and output-gate degrees at most $2^{(b+\varepsilon)n}$. Glue these circuits together by taking their concatenation sum. A fixed input or output gate is involved in only polynomially many summands of the concatenation sum. Hence the resulting circuit has maximum input-output degree at most $2^{(\phi(\mathcal C)+\varepsilon)n+o(n)}$. Letting $n\to\infty$ and then $\varepsilon\downarrow0$ gives $\delta(M)\le 2^{\phi(\mathcal C)}$. For a full proof, see Appendix B.2. $\square$

To see how $\mathcal{C}$ performs at different pairs of probability distributions $(p,q)$, define
$$
\phi(\mathcal{C},p,q):=\min_{(a,b)\in\operatorname{conv}\{P_C(p,q):C\in\mathcal C\}}\max\{a,b\}.
$$
We call the map $(p,q)\mapsto \phi(\mathcal{C},p,q)$ from $\Delta_{[\mathfrak{c}_0]}\times\Delta_{[\mathfrak{c}_1]}$ to $\mathbb{R}_{\geq0}$ the *degree landscape* of $\mathcal{C}$.

### Section 2.3.3: Instantiation of the Transfer Theorems

For each of the four circuit families $\mathrm X\in\{\mathrm{Jessica},\mathrm{Sonetto},\mathrm{Regulus},\mathrm{Regulus}^{\mathsf T}\}$, Section 3 will define a function $\ell_{\mathrm X}:[0,1]\to\mathbb R_{\ge0}$.

By Proposition 3.8, for $m_*:=10^{11}$, for every $m\ge m_*$, every $h\in[H]$, and every $p\in[0,1]$, the following inequalities hold:
$$
\begin{aligned}
f_{C_{\mathrm{Jessica}}(7m,h)}(\operatorname{Ber}(p))&\le\ell_{\mathrm{Jessica}}(p),\\
f_{C_{\mathrm{Sonetto}}(7m,h)}(\operatorname{Ber}(p))&\le\ell_{\mathrm{Sonetto}}(p),\\
f_{C_{\mathrm{Regulus}}(7m,h)}(\operatorname{Ber}(p))&\le\ell_{\mathrm{Regulus}}(p),\\
f_{C_{\mathrm{Regulus}^{\mathsf T}}(7m,h)}(\operatorname{Ber}(p))&\le\ell_{\mathrm{Regulus}^{\mathsf T}}(p).
\end{aligned}
$$

By the definitions of $C_{\mathrm J},C_{\mathrm S},C_{\mathrm R},C_{\mathrm{R}^{\mathsf T}}$ and Proposition 2.2, for all $p,q\in[0,1]$,
$$
\begin{aligned}
f_{C_{\mathrm J}}(\operatorname{Ber}(p))&\le\ell_{\mathrm{Jessica}}(p),&
g_{C_{\mathrm J}}(\operatorname{Ber}(q))&\le\ell_{\mathrm{Jessica}}(q),\\
f_{C_{\mathrm S}}(\operatorname{Ber}(p))&\le\ell_{\mathrm{Sonetto}}(p),&
g_{C_{\mathrm S}}(\operatorname{Ber}(q))&\le\ell_{\mathrm{Sonetto}}(q),\\
f_{C_{\mathrm R}}(\operatorname{Ber}(p))&\le\ell_{\mathrm{Regulus}}(p),&
g_{C_{\mathrm R}}(\operatorname{Ber}(q))&\le\ell_{\mathrm{Regulus}^{\mathsf T}}(q),\\
f_{C_{\mathrm{R}^{\mathsf T}}}(\operatorname{Ber}(p))&\le\ell_{\mathrm{Regulus}^{\mathsf T}}(p),&
g_{C_{\mathrm{R}^{\mathsf T}}}(\operatorname{Ber}(q))&\le\ell_{\mathrm{Regulus}}(q).
\end{aligned}
$$

We first instantiate the size transfer theorem on the singleton set $\{C_{\mathrm J}\}$. By Theorem 2.6,
$$
\begin{aligned}
\sigma(R_1)\le2^{\xi(\{C_{\mathrm J}\})},\qquad\xi(\{C_{\mathrm J}\})&=
\max_{(p,q)\in\Delta_{\{0,1\}}\times\Delta_{\{0,1\}}}\left\{
\min_{(a,b)\in\{P_{C_{\mathrm J}}(p,q)\}}
\max\{a+\mathrm H_2(p),b+\mathrm H_2(q)\}\right\}\\
&=\max\left\{\max_{p\in[0,1]}\{f_{C_{\mathrm J}}(\operatorname{Ber}(p))+\mathrm h(p)\},\max_{q\in[0,1]}\{g_{C_{\mathrm J}}(\operatorname{Ber}(q))+\mathrm h(q)\}\right\}\\
&\le\max_{p\in[0,1]}\{\ell_{\mathrm{Jessica}}(p)+\mathrm h(p)\}.
\end{aligned}
$$
We claim
$$
\max_{p\in[0,1]}\{\ell_{\mathrm{Jessica}}(p)+\mathrm h(p)\}<1.244844.
\tag{$\boldsymbol{*}$}
$$
In Section 3.3.2, we will define a program $\mathcal{V}_\text{size}$ called the *size-inequality verifier*, and Theorem 3.9 proves that if $\mathcal{V}_\text{size}$ accepts, then $(\boldsymbol{*})$ must hold.

**Corollary 2.8 (Upper Bound on $\sigma(R_1)$).** $\sigma(R_1)<2^{1.244844}$.

*Proof of Corollary 2.8.* This follows from Theorem 3.9 and the fact that we ran $\mathcal{V}_\text{size}$ and it accepts. $\square$

For the five circuits $C_0,\ldots,C_4$ defined in Section 2.2, a direct computation gives
$$
\begin{aligned}
f_{C_0}(\operatorname{Ber}(p))&=0,&g_{C_0}(\operatorname{Ber}(q))&=1-q,\\
f_{C_1}(\operatorname{Ber}(p))&=1-p,&g_{C_1}(\operatorname{Ber}(q))&=0,\\
f_{C_2}(\operatorname{Ber}(p))&=p,&g_{C_2}(\operatorname{Ber}(q))&=q,\\
f_{C_3}(\operatorname{Ber}(p))&=p(1-p),&g_{C_3}(\operatorname{Ber}(q))&=\tfrac12(1-q^2),\\
f_{C_4}(\operatorname{Ber}(p))&=\tfrac12(1-p^2),&g_{C_4}(\operatorname{Ber}(q))&=q(1-q).
\end{aligned}
$$
For the three circuits $C_{\mathrm S},C_{\mathrm R},C_{\mathrm{R}^{\mathsf T}}$, we have
$$
\begin{aligned}
f_{C_{\mathrm S}}(\operatorname{Ber}(p))&\le\ell_{\mathrm{Sonetto}}(p),&
g_{C_{\mathrm S}}(\operatorname{Ber}(q))&\le\ell_{\mathrm{Sonetto}}(q),\\
f_{C_{\mathrm R}}(\operatorname{Ber}(p))&\le\ell_{\mathrm{Regulus}}(p),&
g_{C_{\mathrm R}}(\operatorname{Ber}(q))&\le\ell_{\mathrm{Regulus}^{\mathsf T}}(q),\\
f_{C_{\mathrm{R}^{\mathsf T}}}(\operatorname{Ber}(p))&\le\ell_{\mathrm{Regulus}^{\mathsf T}}(p),&
g_{C_{\mathrm{R}^{\mathsf T}}}(\operatorname{Ber}(q))&\le\ell_{\mathrm{Regulus}}(q).
\end{aligned}
$$
Let $\mathcal C_8:=
\{C_0,C_1,C_2,C_3,C_4,C_{\mathrm S},C_{\mathrm R},C_{\mathrm{R}^{\mathsf T}}\}$ be the collection of eight circuits used for the degree-transfer-theorem instantiation below.

Define eight points in the first quadrant of the Cartesian plane by
$$
\begin{aligned}
Q_0(p,q)&:=(0,1-q),\\
Q_1(p,q)&:=(1-p,0),\\
Q_2(p,q)&:=(p,q),\\
Q_3(p,q)&:=(p(1-p),\tfrac12(1-q^2)),\\
Q_4(p,q)&:=(\tfrac12(1-p^2),q(1-q)),\\
Q_5(p,q)&:=(\ell_{\mathrm{Sonetto}}(p),\ell_{\mathrm{Sonetto}}(q)),\\
Q_6(p,q)&:=(\ell_{\mathrm{Regulus}}(p),\ell_{\mathrm{Regulus}^{\mathsf T}}(q)),\\
Q_7(p,q)&:=(\ell_{\mathrm{Regulus}^{\mathsf T}}(p),\ell_{\mathrm{Regulus}}(q)),
\end{aligned}
$$
and set
$$
\mathbf z(p,q)
:=
\min_{(a,b)\in\operatorname{conv}\{Q_0(p,q),\dots,Q_7(p,q)\}}\max\{a,b\}.
$$

We now instantiate the degree transfer theorem on the set $\mathcal C_8$. By Theorem 2.7,
$$
\begin{aligned}
\delta(R_1)\le2^{\phi(\mathcal C_8)},\qquad\phi(\mathcal C_8)&=
\max_{(p,q)\in\Delta_{\{0,1\}}\times\Delta_{\{0,1\}}}\left\{
\min_{(a,b)\in\operatorname{conv}\{P_C(p,q):C\in\mathcal C_8\}}\max\{a,b\}\right\}\\
&\le\max_{(p,q)\in[0,1]^2}\mathbf z(p,q).
\end{aligned}
$$
We claim
$$
\max_{(p,q)\in[0,1]^2}\mathbf z(p,q)<0.319866.
\tag{$\boldsymbol{**}$}
$$
In Section 3.3.3, we will define a program $\mathcal{V}_\text{degree}$ called the *degree-inequality verifier*, and Theorem 3.10 proves that if $\mathcal{V}_\text{degree}$ accepts, then $(\boldsymbol{**})$ must hold.

**Corollary 2.9 (Upper Bound on $\delta(R_1)$).** $\delta(R_1)<2^{0.319866}$.

*Proof of Corollary 2.9.* This follows from Theorem 3.10 and the fact that we ran $\mathcal{V}_\text{degree}$ and it accepts. $\square$

*Remark. Examining $\ell_{\mathrm{Jessica}}(p)+\mathrm h(p)$ for $p\in[0,1]$ and $\mathbf z(p,q)$ for $(p,q)\in[0,1]^2$ reveals specific bottlenecks for future optimizations: to improve the upper bound on $\sigma(R_1)$, one should design a circuit $C$ that minimizes $\frac{f_C(\operatorname{Ber}(p_0))+g_C(\operatorname{Ber}(p_0))}{2}$ near $p_0\approx0.37$; to improve the upper bound on $\delta(R_1)$, one should design a circuit $C$ such that $\frac{f_C(\operatorname{Ber}(p_1))+g_C(\operatorname{Ber}(p_1))}{2}<p_1$ for $p_1$ as small as possible, with the relevant range here being $p_1\approx0.319$.*

Recall that for an integer $c\ge2$, the $c$-ary partial-match incidence matrix is
$$
P_1^{(c)}=\begin{pmatrix}I_c&\mathbf 1_c\end{pmatrix}=
\begin{pmatrix}
1&0&\cdots&0&1\\
0&1&\cdots&0&1\\
\vdots&\vdots&\ddots&\vdots&\vdots\\
0&0&\cdots&1&1
\end{pmatrix}.
$$
We now specify the $c+2$ circuits $C'_{\textsf{pull}},C'_{\textsf{push}},C'_{(0)},\ldots,C'_{(c-1)}$ mentioned in Section 1.2.

|                                   Circuit | # Middle Gates |                                        $L_C(x)$ |                                        $R_C(y)$ | $f_C(p)$ |      $g_C(q)$ |
| ----------------------------------------: | -------------: | ----------------------------------------------: | ----------------------------------------------: | -------: | ------------: |
|     $C'_{\textsf{pull}}:=(I_c,P_1^{(c)})$ |            $c$ |                                            $=1$ | $=\begin{cases}1,&y\neq c,\\ c,&y=c\end{cases}$ |      $0$ | $q_c\log_2 c$ |
| $C'_{\textsf{push}}:=(P_1^{(c)},I_{c+1})$ |          $c+1$ |                                            $=2$ |                                            $=1$ |      $1$ |           $0$ |
|           $C'_{(i)}:=(A'_{(i)},B'_{(i)})$ |            $c$ | $=\begin{cases}2,&x\neq i,\\ 1,&x=i\end{cases}$ | $=\begin{cases}1,&y\neq i,\\ c,&y=i\end{cases}$ |  $1-p_i$ | $q_i\log_2 c$ |

Here $A'_{(i)}:=I_c+(\mathbf 1_c-e_i)e_i^{\mathsf T}$ and $B'_{(i)}:=\begin{pmatrix}I_c-(\mathbf 1_c-e_i)e_i^{\mathsf T}&e_i\end{pmatrix}$. Equivalently:

- In $C'_{\textsf{pull}}$, middle gate $j$ receives weight $1$ from input gate $j$ and sends weight $1$ to output gates $j$ and $c$.
- In $C'_{\textsf{push}}$, for $j<c$, middle gate $j$ receives weight $1$ from input gate $j$ and sends weight $1$ to output gate $j$; middle gate $c$ receives weight $1$ from every input gate and sends weight $1$ to output gate $c$.
- In $C'_{(i)}$, for $j\neq i$, middle gate $j$ receives weight $1$ from input gate $j$, sends weight $1$ to output gate $j$, and sends weight $-1$ to output gate $i$; middle gate $i$ receives weight $1$ from every input gate and sends weight $1$ to output gates $i$ and $c$.


It can be checked that $I_cP_1^{(c)}=P_1^{(c)}$ and $P_1^{(c)}I_{c+1}=P_1^{(c)}$. Also, since $e_i^{\mathsf T}(\mathbf 1_c-e_i)=0$ and $e_i^{\mathsf T}e_i=1$,
$$
\begin{aligned}
A'_{(i)}B'_{(i)}
&=\left(I_c+(\mathbf 1_c-e_i)e_i^{\mathsf T}\right)
\begin{pmatrix}I_c-(\mathbf 1_c-e_i)e_i^{\mathsf T}&e_i\end{pmatrix} \\
&=
\begin{pmatrix}
I_c-(\mathbf 1_c-e_i)e_i^{\mathsf T}
+(\mathbf 1_c-e_i)e_i^{\mathsf T}
-(\mathbf 1_c-e_i)\bigl(e_i^{\mathsf T}(\mathbf 1_c-e_i)\bigr)e_i^{\mathsf T}
&
e_i+(\mathbf 1_c-e_i)(e_i^{\mathsf T}e_i)
\end{pmatrix} \\
&=\begin{pmatrix}I_c&\mathbf 1_c\end{pmatrix}
=P_1^{(c)}.
\end{aligned}
$$
Finally, we instantiate the size transfer theorem on the set $\{C'_{\textsf{pull}},C'_{\textsf{push}}\}$ and the degree transfer theorem on the set $\{C'_{\textsf{pull}},C'_{(0)},\ldots,C'_{(c-1)}\}$.

**Theorem 2.10 (Upper Bounds on $\sigma(P_1^{(c)})$ and on $\delta(P_1^{(c)})$).** For every integer $c\ge2$,
$$
\sigma(P_1^{(c)})\leq c\cdot\psi(\log_2c),
\qquad
\delta(P_1^{(c)})\leq c^{\frac{c-1}{(c-1)+c\log_2c}},
$$
where $\psi(r)$ is the unique root $x>1$ of $x=1+\frac{1}{x^r}$.

*Proof of Theorem 2.10.* Let $x:=\psi(\log_2 c)$, so that $x=1+\frac{1}{x^{\log_2 c}}$. For the upper bound on $\sigma(P_1^{(c)})$, apply Theorem 2.6 to $\{C'_{\textsf{pull}},C'_{\textsf{push}}\}$. At $(p,q)$, the two manifestation points are $(0,q_c\log_2 c)$ and $(1,0)$. We choose the point $(a,b)=(\log_2 x,(1-\log_2 x)q_c\log_2 c)$ on their line segment. Using $\mathrm H_c(p)\le \log_2 c$, $\mathrm H_{c+1}(q)\le \mathrm h(q_c)+(1-q_c)\log_2 c$, and $\max_t\{\mathrm h(t)-t(\log_2 c)(\log_2 x)\}=\log_2\left(1+\frac{1}{x^{\log_2 c}}\right)=\log_2 x$, we get $\xi\le \log_2 c+\log_2 x$. Hence $\sigma(P_1^{(c)})\le 2^{\log_2 c+\log_2 x}=c\cdot\psi(\log_2 c)$.

For the upper bound on $\delta(P_1^{(c)})$, apply Theorem 2.7 to $\{C'_{\textsf{pull}},C'_{(0)},\ldots,C'_{(c-1)}\}$. At $(p,q)$, the $c+1$ manifestation points are $(0,q_c\log_2 c)$ and $(1-p_i,q_i\log_2 c)$ for $i=0,\ldots,c-1$. We choose the point on their convex hull obtained from the weights $\frac{c-1}{(c-1)+c\log_2 c},\frac{\log_2 c}{(c-1)+c\log_2 c},\ldots,\frac{\log_2 c}{(c-1)+c\log_2 c}$. Using $\sum_i p_i=1$, $\sum_i q_i=1-q_c$, and $\log_2 c\le c-1$, this point has both coordinates at most $\frac{(c-1)\log_2 c}{(c-1)+c\log_2 c}$. Hence $\phi\le \frac{(c-1)\log_2 c}{(c-1)+c\log_2 c}$, and therefore $\delta(P_1^{(c)})\le 2^{\frac{(c-1)\log_2 c}{(c-1)+c\log_2 c}}=c^{\frac{c-1}{(c-1)+c\log_2 c}}$. For a full proof, see Appendix B.2. $\square$
