# Section 3: Lyapunov Exponents and Machine Verification

Let $\mathcal{M}$ be a finite collection of nonnegative matrices, and let $\mu$ be a probability distribution over $\mathcal{M}$. Consider a sequence of independent and identically distributed random matrices $A_1, A_2, \ldots$ drawn from $\mathcal{M}$ according to $\mu$. The associated *quenched Lyapunov exponent* is defined as the almost-sure limit
$$
\lambda=\lim_{m\to\infty}\frac1m\log_2\|A_mA_{m-1}\cdots A_1\|_1,\qquad\text{a.s.}
$$
The existence of this limit is well-established; it is a direct consequence of the Furstenberg–Kesten theorem [FK60] and can be framed within the broader contexts of Kingman's subadditive ergodic theorem [Kin73] or Oseledets' multiplicative ergodic theorem [Ose68]. However, despite this theoretical guarantee of its existence, exactly computing the exponent $\lambda$ remains notoriously difficult.

## Section 3.1: Reducing Mean Log-Degree to Lyapunov Exponent

Fix a parameter set $\mathrm X=(k,H,I,Z,\alpha,\beta)$ in the sense of Section 2.1, and write $I=(\mathbf c,\boldsymbol{\iota})$.

For $x\in\{0,1\}^k$ and $u,v\in[H]$, define
$$
N_{u,v}(x):=\left|\left\{r:\left(U_{c_{\boldsymbol{\iota}(u)},r}\right)_x\neq0,\ \operatorname{clamp}_H\!\left(u+\Delta_{c_{\boldsymbol{\iota}(u)},r}\right)=v\right\}\right|.
$$
By the bit-permutation symmetry of the Sergeev decompositions, $N_{u,v}(x)$ depends only on $\|x\|_1$. Hence, for each $i=0,\ldots,k$, we obtain a matrix $\textsf{A}_i\in\mathbb Z_{\ge0}^{H\times H}$ by setting $\textsf{A}_i[u,v]:=N_{u,v}(x)$ for any $x\in\{0,1\}^k$ with $\|x\|_1=i$. We call $\textsf{A}_0,\ldots,\textsf{A}_k$ the transition matrices of the circuit family generated by $\mathrm X$.

**Lemma 3.1.** The transition matrices $\textsf{A}_0,\ldots,\textsf{A}_k$ are well-defined.

*Proof of Lemma 3.1.* See Appendix B.3. $\square$

**Proposition 3.2.** For every $m\ge1$, every $h\in[H]$, and every input-gate index $x\in\{0,1\}^{km}$, write $x$ as $m$ consecutive $k$-bit blocks $x=(x^{(1)},\ldots,x^{(m)})$, and put $i_t:=\|x^{(t)}\|_1$. Then
$$
L_{C_{\mathrm X}(km,h)}(x)=\left\|e_h^{\mathsf T}\textsf{A}_{i_1}\cdots \textsf{A}_{i_m}\right\|_1.
$$

*Proof of Proposition 3.2.* Recall that $C_{\mathrm X}(km,h)$ describes a computation tree with $m$ layers. Intuitively, the proposition follows from the following loop invariant: the $v$-th entry of the row vector $e_h^{\mathsf T}\textsf{A}_{i_1}\cdots\textsf{A}_{i_m}$ is the number of nodes in layer $m$ with state $v$ for which the product of the rank-1 terms along the corresponding root-to-node path has a left vector that is nonzero at the input index $x$. Summing these entries over all final states gives the degree of the input gate $x$. For a detailed proof, see Appendix B.3. $\square$

For $p\in[0,1]$, let $\operatorname{Bin}(k,p)$ denote the binomial distribution on $\{0,\ldots,k\}$. Averaging Proposition 3.2 over $X\sim\operatorname{Ber}(p)^{km}$ gives
$$
f_{C_{\mathrm X}(km,h)}(\operatorname{Ber}(p))=\frac1{km}\,\mathbb E_{I_1,\ldots,I_m\sim\operatorname{Bin}(k,p)}\!\left[\log_2\left\|e_h^{\mathsf T}\textsf{A}_{I_1}\cdots \textsf{A}_{I_m}\right\|_1\right],
$$
since the block Hamming weights $I_1,\ldots,I_m$ are i.i.d. $\operatorname{Bin}(k,p)$. Thus the $m\to\infty$ limiting value of $k\cdot f_{C_{\mathrm X}(km,h)}(\operatorname{Ber}(p))$ is not larger than the quenched Lyapunov exponent of the random product drawn from $\textsf{A}_0,\ldots,\textsf{A}_k$ with law $\operatorname{Bin}(k,p)$.

In particular, for the four circuit families used in this paper, the transition matrices can be computed directly from the parameter sets, and one can check that they have the following dimensions and total numbers of nonzero elements in them.

| Family                         | Matrix dimension $H$ | Total nonzeros in $\textsf{A}_0,\ldots,\textsf{A}_7$ |
| ------------------------------ | -------------------: | ---------------------------------------------------: |
| Jessica                        |                $612$ |                                              $13452$ |
| Sonetto                        |                $360$ |                                               $6726$ |
| Regulus                        |                $360$ |                                               $7383$ |
| $\mathrm{Regulus}^{\mathsf T}$ |                $360$ |                                               $6772$ |

## Section 3.2: Toll-and-Potential Certificates and Lattice Envelopes

**Definition 3.3 (Toll-and-Potential Certificates).** A *toll-and-potential certificate* for transition matrices $\textsf{A}_0,\ldots,\textsf{A}_k$ is an ordered pair $(\mathfrak{a},\mathfrak{b})$ of arrays $\mathfrak{a}=\{\mathfrak{a}_{i,j}\}_{0\le i,j\le k}$ and $\mathfrak{b}=\{\mathfrak{b}_{i,u}\}_{0\le i\le k,\,u\in[H]}$. The pair is called an $\varepsilon$-valid certificate if, for every $i=0,\ldots,k$ and every $u\in[H]$,
$$
\sum_{j=0}^k\sum_{v=0}^{H-1}\textsf{A}_j[u,v]\,2^{-\mathfrak{a}_{i,j}-\mathfrak{b}_{i,u}+\mathfrak{b}_{j,v}}\le1-\varepsilon.
$$

For $p\in[0,1]$, write $\operatorname{Bin}(k,p)_i:=\binom{k}{i}p^i(1-p)^{k-i}$, and define the *binomial entropy*
$$
E_k(p):=-\sum_{i=0}^k\operatorname{Bin}(k,p)_i\log_2\operatorname{Bin}(k,p)_i,\qquad0\log_2 0:=0.
$$
**Definition 3.4 (Crude/Refined Evaluations).** For $\mathfrak{a}=\{\mathfrak{a}_{i,j}\}_{0\le i,j\le k}$, define its *crude evaluation* at $p$ by
$$
\Phi_{\mathfrak{a}}(p):=\sum_{i=0}^k\sum_{j=0}^k\operatorname{Bin}(k,p)_i\operatorname{Bin}(k,p)_j\,\mathfrak{a}_{i,j},
$$
and its *refined evaluation* at $p$ by $\widetilde\Phi_{\mathfrak{a}}(p):=\frac1k(\Phi_{\mathfrak{a}}(p)-E_k(p))$.

For $0<\eta\le0.5$, define the *$\eta$-clipped binomial entropy*
$$
\underline{E}_{k,\eta}(p):=\begin{cases}
E_k(\eta)\cdot\frac{p}{\eta},&0\le p\le\eta,\\
E_k(p),&\eta\le p\le1-\eta,\\
E_k(\eta)\cdot\frac{1-p}{\eta},&1-\eta\le p\le1.
\end{cases}
$$
For $\mathfrak{a}=\{\mathfrak{a}_{i,j}\}_{0\le i,j\le k}$, define its *$\eta$-clipped refined evaluation* by $\widetilde{\widetilde\Phi}_{\mathfrak{a},\eta}(p):=\frac1k(\Phi_{\mathfrak{a}}(p)-\underline{E}_{k,\eta}(p))$.

Since $E_k$ is concave, $\underline{E}_{k,\eta}(p)\le E_k(p)$ for all $p$, and therefore $\widetilde{\widetilde\Phi}_{\mathfrak{a},\eta}(p)\ge\widetilde\Phi_{\mathfrak{a}}(p)$.

**Theorem 3.5 (Certificate Theorem).** Suppose that, for some $0<\varepsilon<1$, $(\mathfrak{a},\mathfrak{b})$ is an $\varepsilon$-valid toll-and-potential certificate for the transition matrices $\textsf{A}_0,\ldots,\textsf{A}_k$ of the circuit family generated by $\mathrm X$. Set
$$
\Gamma(\mathfrak{b}):=\max_{i,u}\mathfrak{b}_{i,u}-\min_{i,u}\mathfrak{b}_{i,u}.
$$
Then, for every $m$, $h$, and $p$,
$$
f_{C_{\mathrm X}(km,h)}(\operatorname{Ber}(p))\le\widetilde\Phi_{\mathfrak{a}}(p)+\frac{\Gamma(\mathfrak{b})+m\log_2(1-\varepsilon)}{km}.
$$
In particular, if $m\ge\left\lceil\Gamma(\mathfrak{b})/(-\log_2(1-\varepsilon))\right\rceil$, then $f_{C_{\mathrm X}(km,h)}(\operatorname{Ber}(p))\le\widetilde\Phi_{\mathfrak{a}}(p)\le\widetilde{\widetilde\Phi}_{\mathfrak{a},\eta}(p)$ uniformly over $h$ and $p$, for every $0<\eta\le0.5$.

*Proof of Theorem 3.5.* See Appendix B.3. $\square$

For a fixed $p$, a smaller value of $\Phi_{\mathfrak{a}}(p)$ gives a better upper bound. In fact, minimizing $\Phi_{\mathfrak{a}}(p)$ subject to $(\mathfrak{a},\mathfrak{b})$ being a $0$-valid certificate is a convex minimization problem [[footnote 1]]. In the i.i.d. case, this convex program is the Lagrangian dual of the convex program of Gharavi and Anantharam [GA05] [[footnote 2]]. Here, however, Theorem 3.5 is proved directly in Appendix B.3, and the correctness of [GA05] is not needed for the correctness of this paper.

**Theorem 3.6 (Lattice Envelopes).** Fix a parameter set $\mathrm X$ and a finite lattice $0=p_0<p_1<\cdots<p_L=1$. Fix $\eta$ and $\varepsilon$ with $0<\eta\le0.5$ and $0<\varepsilon<1$. Suppose that, for each interior lattice point $p_r$, $r=1,\ldots,L-1$, we have an $\varepsilon$-valid certificate $(\mathfrak{a}^{(r)},\mathfrak{b}^{(r)})$.

For notational convenience, introduce formal boundary symbols $\mathfrak{a}^{(0)}$ and $\mathfrak{a}^{(L)}$ by declaring
$$
\widetilde{\widetilde\Phi}_{\mathfrak{a}^{(0)},\eta}(p)
=
\widetilde{\widetilde\Phi}_{\mathfrak{a}^{(L)},\eta}(p)
:=+\infty
\qquad\text{for every }p\in[0,1].
$$
Define $\ell_{\mathrm X}:[0,1]\to\mathbb R_{\ge0}$ by
$$
\ell_{\mathrm X}(p):=
\min\left\{
\widetilde{\widetilde\Phi}_{\mathfrak{a}^{(r)},\eta}(p),
\widetilde{\widetilde\Phi}_{\mathfrak{a}^{(r+1)},\eta}(p)
\right\},
\qquad
p\in[p_r,p_{r+1}),\quad r=0,\ldots,L-1,
$$
where the last interval is understood to include its right endpoint $p_L=1$. Let
$$
m_{\mathrm X}:=\max_{1\le r\le L-1}\left\lceil\frac{\Gamma(\mathfrak{b}^{(r)})}{-\log_2(1-\varepsilon)}\right\rceil.
$$
Then, for every $m\ge m_{\mathrm X}$, $f_{C_{\mathrm X}(km,h)}(\operatorname{Ber}(p))\le\ell_{\mathrm X}(p)$ uniformly over $h$ and $p$.

Moreover, if, at every interior lattice point $p_r$, $r=1,\ldots,L-1$,
$$
\widetilde{\widetilde\Phi}_{\mathfrak{a}^{(r)},\eta}(p_r)
\le
\min\left\{
\widetilde{\widetilde\Phi}_{\mathfrak{a}^{(r-1)},\eta}(p_r),
\widetilde{\widetilde\Phi}_{\mathfrak{a}^{(r+1)},\eta}(p_r)
\right\},
$$
then $\ell_{\mathrm X}$ is continuous on $[0,1]$. These inequalities will be referred to as the *adjacent-certificate conditions*.

*Proof of Theorem 3.6.* See Appendix B.3. $\square$

Finally, define the four lattice envelopes $\ell_{\mathrm{Jessica}}$, $\ell_{\mathrm{Sonetto}}$, $\ell_{\mathrm{Regulus}}$, and $\ell_{\mathrm{Regulus}^{\mathsf T}}$ by applying Theorem 3.6 with $\eta=0.01$ and $\varepsilon=10^{-8}$, using the family-specific lattices listed below, and the certificates stored in the verification repository at https://github.com/leafseek-research-code/ye26-verification-pipeline.

In the descriptions below, $a\xrightarrow{\;\delta\;}b$ means that the lattice includes all points $a,a+\delta,a+2\delta,\ldots,b$.

For Jessica, the lattice, consisting of $L_\mathrm{Jessica}=290$ subintervals, is given by

$$
For Sonetto, the lattice, consisting of $L_\mathrm{Sonetto}=370$ subintervals, is given by

\xrightarrow{\;0.01\;}0.2

$$
For Regulus, the lattice, consisting of $L_\mathrm{Regulus}=208$ subintervals, is given by

## Section 3.3: Machine Verification of the Upper Bounds

This section explains how the two claims used in Section 2.3.3 are verified. The verification uses three deterministic programs: the information inspector $\mathcal{I}$, the size-inequality verifier $\mathcal{V}_\text{size}$, and the degree-inequality verifier $\mathcal{V}_\text{degree}$. We prove on paper that if these programs accept, then the two inequalities needed for Corollaries 2.8 and 2.9 hold; we then run the programs and observe that they do accept.

### Section 3.3.1: Lipschitz-Continuity of the Lattice Envelopes

Let $\mathrm X$ be one of the four circuit families, and let $\{(\mathfrak{a}^{(r)},\mathfrak{b}^{(r)})\}_{r=1}^{L-1}$ be the certificates defining $\ell_{\mathrm X}$. Since $k=7$ for all four parameter sets, $\widetilde{\widetilde\Phi}_{\mathfrak{a}^{(r)},0.01}(p)=\frac17(\Phi_{\mathfrak{a}^{(r)}}(p)-\underline{E}_{7,0.01}(p))$. Also,
$$
\Phi_{\mathfrak{a}^{(r)}}(p)=\sum_{i=0}^7\sum_{j=0}^7\mathfrak{a}^{(r)}_{i,j}\binom7i\binom7j p^{i+j}(1-p)^{14-i-j}.
$$
Equivalently, in Bernstein form,
$$
\Phi_{\mathfrak{a}^{(r)}}(p)=\sum_{t=0}^{14}\overline{\mathfrak{a}}^{(r)}_t\binom{14}{t}p^t(1-p)^{14-t},\qquad
\overline{\mathfrak{a}}^{(r)}_t:=\sum_{i=\max(0,t-7)}^{\min(7,t)}\frac{\binom7i\binom7{t-i}}{\binom{14}{t}}\,\mathfrak{a}^{(r)}_{i,t-i}.
$$

**Proposition 3.7 (Lipschitz Continuity).** For a fixed certificate, $\widetilde{\widetilde\Phi}_{\mathfrak{a}^{(r)},0.01}$ is Lipschitz continuous on $[0,1]$, with Lipschitz constant
$$
\operatorname{Lip}^{(r)}:=\frac17\left(14\max_{0\le t<14}\lvert\overline{\mathfrak{a}}^{(r)}_{t+1}-\overline{\mathfrak{a}}^{(r)}_t\rvert+\frac{E_7(0.01)}{0.01}\right).
$$
Moreover, if the adjacent-certificate condition in Theorem 3.6 holds at every interior lattice point, then $\ell_{\mathrm X}$ is Lipschitz continuous on $[0,1]$ with Lipschitz constant $\operatorname{Lip}_{\mathrm X}:=\max_r\operatorname{Lip}^{(r)}$.

*Proof of Proposition 3.7.* See Appendix B.3. $\square$

For each $\mathrm X\in\{\mathrm{Jessica},\mathrm{Sonetto},\mathrm{Regulus},\mathrm{Regulus}^{\mathsf T}\}$, the information inspector $\mathcal{I}$ performs the following checks. First, for every $r,i,u$, it computes $S^{(r)}_{i,u}:=\sum_{j,v}\textsf{A}_j[u,v]2^{-\mathfrak{a}^{(r)}_{i,j}-\mathfrak{b}^{(r)}_{i,u}+\mathfrak{b}^{(r)}_{j,v}}$. Second, for every $r,t$, it computes the Bernstein coefficient $\overline{\mathfrak{a}}^{(r)}_t$. Third, for every interior lattice point, it checks the adjacent-certificate condition from Theorem 3.6. If any check fails, $\mathcal{I}$ rejects immediately.

Running $\mathcal{I}$ gives the following global information across all four circuit families:
$$
\begin{aligned}
\max_{r,i,u}S^{(r)}_{i,u} && 0.9999999884\\
\max_{r,i,u}\mathfrak{b}^{(r)}_{i,u} && 56.4209\\
\min_{r,i,u}\mathfrak{b}^{(r)}_{i,u} && -55.7883\\
\max_{r,t}\left|\overline{\mathfrak{a}}^{(r)}_{t+1}-\overline{\mathfrak{a}}^{(r)}_t\right| && 3.354665\\
E_7(0.01) && 0.371591\\[4pt]
\text{All checks pass?} && \text{True}
\end{aligned}
$$
The numbers above are truncated to the displayed precision.

**Proposition 3.8.** The following two statements hold.

1. For each $\mathrm X$, the inequality $f_{C_{\mathrm X}(7m,h)}(\operatorname{Ber}(p))\le\ell_{\mathrm X}(p)$ holds for all $m\ge10^{11}$, $h\in[H]$, and $p\in[0,1]$.
2. For each $\mathrm X$, the function $\ell_{\mathrm X}$ is $13$-Lipschitz on $[0,1]$.

*Proof of Proposition 3.8.* See Appendix B.3. $\square$

### Section 3.3.2: Verification of the Size Upper Bound

The size-inequality verifier $\mathcal{V}_\text{size}$ is a deterministic program that proves, if it accepts, that
$$
\max_{p\in[0,1]}\{\ell_{\mathrm{Jessica}}(p)+\mathrm h(p)\}\le1.244843.
$$
This is stronger than the size inequality $(\boldsymbol{*})$ from Section 2.3.3.

The program uses the Jessica lattice $0=p_0<p_1<\cdots<p_L=1$. It partitions each lattice interval $[p_i,p_{i+1}]$ into $1000$ equal subintervals, with endpoints $p_{i,j}:=p_i+(p_{i+1}-p_i)\cdot\frac{j}{1000}$ for $0\le j\le1000$. For each subinterval $[a,b]$, it computes
$$
M_{[a,b]}:=\ell_{\mathrm{Jessica}}(b)+13(b-a)+\mathrm h(\min\{\max\{1/2,a\},b\}).
$$
The program accepts if $M_{[a,b]}\le1.244843$ for every such subinterval, and rejects otherwise.

**Theorem 3.9 (Soundness of the Size-Inequality Verifier).** If $\mathcal{V}_\text{size}$ accepts, then the size inequality $(\boldsymbol{*})$ from Section 2.3.3 holds.

*Proof of Theorem 3.9.* See Appendix B.3. $\square$

We run $\mathcal{V}_\text{size}$ and observe that it accepts. Hence, by Theorem 3.9, $(\boldsymbol{*})$ holds.

### Section 3.3.3: Verification of the Degree Upper Bound

The degree-inequality verifier $\mathcal{V}_\text{degree}$ is a deterministic program that proves, if it accepts, that
$$
\mathbf z(p,q)\le0.319865\qquad\text{for all }p,q\in[0,1].
$$
This is stronger than the degree inequality $(\boldsymbol{**})$ from Section 2.3.3.

The program defines a boolean test on a rectangle $R=[p_\min,p_\max]\times[q_\min,q_\max]\subseteq[0,1]^2$. At recursion depth $d$, the test does the following:

1. Sample the $101\times101$ grid $\left(\frac{(100-i)p_\min+ip_\max}{100},\frac{(100-j)q_\min+jq_\max}{100}\right)$, for $0\le i,j\le100$.
2. At each grid point $(p_i,q_j)$, form the eight points $Q_{\mathrm{id}}(p_i,q_j)=(x_{\mathrm{id}},y_{\mathrm{id}})$ from Section 2.3.3. Initialize $Z_{i,j}:=\min_{\mathrm{id}}\max\{x_{\mathrm{id}},y_{\mathrm{id}}\}$. For every pair $0\leq\mathrm{hi}<\mathrm{lo}\leq7$, if $(x_{\mathrm{hi}}-y_{\mathrm{hi}})(x_{\mathrm{lo}}-y_{\mathrm{lo}})<0$ and $(x_{\mathrm{hi}}-x_{\mathrm{lo}})(y_{\mathrm{hi}}-y_{\mathrm{lo}})<0$, set $\theta_*:=\frac{x_{\mathrm{hi}}-y_{\mathrm{hi}}}{(x_{\mathrm{hi}}-y_{\mathrm{hi}})-(x_{\mathrm{lo}}-y_{\mathrm{lo}})}$ and update $Z_{i,j}\leftarrow\min\{Z_{i,j},(1-\theta_*)x_{\mathrm{hi}}+\theta_*x_{\mathrm{lo}}\}$.
3. Return true if $Z_{i,j}+13\max\left\{\frac{p_\max-p_\min}{200},\frac{q_\max-q_\min}{200}\right\}\le0.319865$ for every grid point.
4. Otherwise, if $d=30$, return false. If $d\le29$, split $R$ in half along the $p$-axis when $d$ is even and along the $q$-axis when $d$ is odd, apply the same test to both subrectangles at depth $d+1$, and return true exactly when both recursive calls return true.

The program $\mathcal{V}_\text{degree}$ accepts if this test returns true on $[0,1]^2$ at recursion depth $0$.

**Theorem 3.10 (Soundness of the Degree-Inequality Verifier).** If $\mathcal{V}_\text{degree}$ accepts, then the degree inequality $(\boldsymbol{**})$ from Section 2.3.3 holds.

*Proof of Theorem 3.10.* See Appendix B.3. $\square$

We run $\mathcal{V}_\text{degree}$ and observe that it accepts. Hence, by Theorem 3.10, $(\boldsymbol{**})$ holds.

In this run, the verifier makes $2791$ recursive calls, checks $28470991$ grid points, reaches maximum recursion depth $d=24$, and, among all grid points visited, the maximum is $\mathbf z(0.3194995117,0.3202343750)\approx0.3198453628$. At this point, the best pair of coordinate points is $(Q_2,Q_5)$, which corresponds to the two circuits $C_2$ and $C_{\mathrm S}$. On an HPC with $32$ CPU cores, with grid-point evaluation parallelized within each rectangle, the run takes about $45$ minutes.
